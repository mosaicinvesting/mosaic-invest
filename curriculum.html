Module 1

Foundations

By the end, you'll be able to

explain why starting early matters more than starting big, and estimate how long money takes to double.

Compounding is the whole game. It isn't just earning a return, it's earning returns on your past returns, which is why a small amount invested young can outrun a large amount invested late. The biggest lever isn't how much you start with or which stock you pick, it's time.

Saving and investing are different jobs: saving protects money you'll need soon, investing grows money you won't. And cash left sitting isn't automatically safe, because inflation quietly lowers what it can buy each year. One tool you'll reuse forever is the Rule of 72: divide 72 by an annual return to estimate the years to double. At 9%, that's about 8 years.

Checkpoint

At a 9% annual return, roughly how long to double your money?

72 ÷ 9 ≈ 8 years. That's the Rule of 72 in action.

The biggest driver of how much compounding does for you is:

Time in the market is the biggest lever, more than the amount or the pick.

Your turn

In one sentence: name one thing you're investing toward, and roughly when you'd need the money.

Module 2

The Investing Toolkit

By the end, you'll be able to

size up a company or fund with a five-question checklist, and read what a fund actually costs you.

Your main building blocks are individual stocks, index funds and ETFs, and bonds. Diversification is why you don't put everything in one name: spreading across many companies means no single failure sinks you. Index funds are the smart default because they buy the whole market cheaply, and most actively managed funds fail to beat the index once fees are counted.

That word fees matters more than it looks. A fund's expense ratio is its annual cost, and the gap between 0.05% and 1.0% quietly compounds into a large chunk of your returns over decades. When you size up a single company, run five questions: What does it do? Is it growing? Does it make money? Is it financially healthy? Is the price reasonable? That last one has a first answer in the P/E ratio, which is roughly how much you pay for each dollar the company earns.

Checkpoint

Two funds track the same index; one charges 1.0%, one charges 0.05%. Over decades, the difference:

Small percentage fees compound against you the same way returns compound for you.

A P/E ratio tells you roughly:

P/E is price divided by earnings per share: the price of one dollar of profit.

In one sentence: name a company or fund you'd research, and the one thing you'd most want to check.

Module 3

Risk and Investor Behavior

By the end, you'll be able to

connect risk, reward, and time horizon, and recognize the behavioral traps that cost investors the most.

Risk and reward are linked: higher potential returns come with bigger swings, and no trick escapes it. Volatility, the size of those swings, is normal, not a sign that something is broken. What makes volatility safe or dangerous is your time horizon: money you won't touch for a decade can ride out drops that would be reckless for money you need next year.

The real damage usually comes from behavior, not markets. Loss aversion means a loss feels about twice as bad as an equal gain feels good, which pushes people to panic-sell at the bottom. Herd behavior does the opposite near the top, piling in because everyone else is. Both are your instincts working against you, and knowing that is half the defense.

Checkpoint

"Loss aversion" means:

Losses feel about twice as strong as equal gains, which is what drives panic-selling.

A 20% drop in a broad index over a year is:

Broad markets fall 20% or more from time to time. It's expected, not a reason to bail.

In one sentence: what would you actually do if your portfolio dropped 20% next month?

Module 4

Getting Set Up to Invest

By the end, you'll be able to

map the steps to open an account, and tell the account types apart.

A brokerage account is where you actually hold investments. As a minor, you can't open one alone: a parent or guardian opens a custodial account and manages it on your behalf. When you buy, order type matters. A market order fills right away at whatever the current price is, while a limit order only fills at your set price or better.

Fractional shares let you buy a slice of an expensive stock, so you can start with small amounts, and you'll want to compare fees across brokerages. Account type is a quieter but huge decision. A regular taxable account is flexible, but a custodial Roth IRA, available if you have earned income, lets qualified growth and later withdrawals come out tax-free, which over decades is worth real money.

Checkpoint

A limit order:

A limit order waits for your price; a market order fills right away at the going price.

For a teen with a summer job, a custodial Roth IRA is appealing because:

With earned income, a Roth grows and pays out tax-free if the rules are met, and decades of compounding make that huge.

Module 5

Building a Portfolio

By the end, you'll be able to

assemble a simple diversified portfolio, and explain the allocation you chose.

Now you combine the building blocks into a whole. Each vehicle plays a role: individual stocks are concentrated bets, index funds and ETFs give instant diversification, target-date funds are an all-in-one option that adjusts over time, and bonds act as ballast that steadies the ride.

The single most important decision is your asset allocation, the split between stocks and bonds. A longer time horizon generally means more in stocks, because you can afford to wait out the swings. Diversification means spreading across companies, sectors, and geographies, which a broad index fund does for you in one purchase. Position sizing is how much you put in any one holding: no single pick should be large enough to sink the whole portfolio. A common shape is core and satellite: a broad index fund as the core, plus a few small individual convictions around it. Over time your winners grow and pull your mix off target, so rebalancing periodically trims it back to your intended weights.

Checkpoint

The biggest driver of a portfolio's long-run risk and return is usually:

Your high-level allocation drives most of the outcome, more than any single holding.

"Core and satellite" means:

The index core does the heavy lifting; small satellites let you express conviction without betting the farm.

Module 6

Building the Long-Term Habit

By the end, you'll be able to

design an investing routine you can actually keep for a year.

The hardest part of investing isn't picking, it's staying. Learn to read a portfolio without obsessing over it, and judge a decision by whether it was sound at the time, not by a short-term result you couldn't control. Price swings are normal and rarely a reason to act.

The engine of the long game is dollar-cost averaging: investing a fixed amount on a regular schedule regardless of price, which removes the pressure to time the market. Make it effortless with automatic recurring contributions so the habit runs without willpower. And watch the two slow leaks, fees and taxes, which compound against you over the years the same way returns compound for you, so low costs and not overtrading quietly matter as much as any single pick.

Checkpoint

Dollar-cost averaging means:

You invest the same amount on a schedule regardless of price, which removes the timing guesswork.

A good habit for most long-term investors is to:

Automate the boring part and check in occasionally. Reacting to every headline usually hurts.
